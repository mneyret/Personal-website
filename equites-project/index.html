EquitES project

Context


Ecosystems and biodiversity contribute to our society in numerous ways — from the production of food and timber to the regulation of air and water quality, as well as through outdoor recreation opportunities and the cultural value of species and landscapes. These benefits, also known as ecosystem services or nature’s contributions to people (NCP), are fundamental to human well-being.

However, access to these contributions and the ability to benefit from them vary among individuals depending on their geographic location, personal preferences, and mobility. As a result, significant disparities may arise between social groups, particularly in terms of age, gender, and income.

Climate change further compounds these challenges, threatening the management and conservation of ecosystems and altering their capacity to provide benefits to society. For instance, the increasing frequency of droughts and heatwaves may lead to reduced food and fodder production in many European regions. Yet, how these changes affect nature’s contributions to different people — and how they are distributed among social groups — remains largely uncertain.

The EquitES project aims to better understand how climate change impacts nature’s contributions across different social groups.

More specifically, our objectives are to:

  • Identify the key socio-demographic factors driving disparities in access to, and benefits derived from, nature — and assess their impact on quality of life;
  
  • Evaluate how different climate change scenarios may influence these disparities;

  • Develop nature-based solutions that help mitigate these impacts while preserving biodiversity.

Our study area covers the broader Grenoble region in France.


Project Activities and Results

The project ran from October 2023 to November 2025. The results are still being finalised and this page will be updated more results become available.

Disparities in Urban NCP in the Grenoble metropolis

Key message: In the Grenoble area, marginalized urban population have a good access to green spaces of relateively high quality. However, strong disparities subsist in terms of climate risks, such as heatwave mitigation and protection from floods by the vegetation.

Dans la région grenobloise, les populations urbaines vulnérables ont un accès raisonnable à des espaces verts de bonne qualité. Toutefois, des inégalités persistent en ce qui concerne la régulation des risques liés au changement climatique, comme la régulation de la température et la protection contre les inondations par la végétation.

The first study from this project focused on the main urban area of the study region, the Grenoble Metropolis. Urban green spaces provide many important benefits - from helping to reduce heat and flooding risks to offering places for recreation and relaxation. These  are essential for the well-being of city residents. However, these benefits are not shared equally across social groups. Environmental segregation, meaning the separation of population groups between areas with different environmental quality, is still common. Research in environmental justice has shown that low-income neighborhoods often have less and lower-quality green space. Yet, to support fairer urban planning, we need more detailed assessments that consider different types of ecosystem services and the diverse needs of communities.

In this study, we combined socio-demographic spatial data with NCP spatial modeling to examine inequalities in the distribution of three key services — heatwave mitigation, flood regulation, and urban recreation — in the Grenoble metropolitan area. For the urban recreation service, we created a novel recreation index that combines the quality and accessibility of urban green spaces.


These findings underline the need to integrate social needs and vulnerabilities into urban greening strategies. Urban policies that go beyond simply increasing green space, and instead focus on delivering multiple, equitable NCP, can help ensure that all communities share in the benefits of a greener, more resilient city.

The study has been published online as a preprint and is currently under review.

Overview of survey results

We conducted a survey between March and october 2024 among the general public of the study region, with the aim to identify the nature's contributions to people most prioritised by different persons, identify which natural and green spaces provide them, and the barriers limiting access to those benefits.

Around 2000 people participated to the survey, and a bit more than 700 finished the whole questionnaire. participants were relatively balanced in terms of gender; intellectual professions, retired people and students were the most common. Many participants live in the Grenoble Metropolis, but around 53% lived in the rural or mountainous areas of the study region. 

Barriers to Nature's Contributions to People

Key message: the barriers that limit access to nature and its contributions to quality of life are diverse. They include the low availability or quality of green and natural spaces, but also overcrowding, as well as lack of time or knowledge. Our survey show that people who live in rural areas, men, people with good physical mobility and high income report fewer barriers. Having less limitations was associated to a better quality of life and a recognition of nature's contributions to their well-being. 

Nature's contributions to our quality of life - from production of resources to regulation of temperatures or recreational activities - are not shared equally across the population. Various barriers can limit access to them: physical barriers (poor quality of green spaces, limited access or transportation), legal barriers (private ownership), as well as social and individual barriers (feelings of insecurity, lack of time, social isolation). Understanding these barriers across social groups, as well as their effects on connection to nature (the sense of belonging to and closeness with the natural world around us) and on quality of life, is therefore a key lever for guiding actions aimed at ensuring equitable access to green and natural spaces. To address this issue, we conducted a survey between March and October 2024 among residents of the Isère department (France). 

Barriers to access to nature

The most frequently reported barriers depend on the type of contribution. Access to non-material contributions (cultural value, recreation) is mainly limited by lack of time and overcrowding of natural areas. Constraints related to material contributions (access to timber and local agricultural products) primarily involve a lack of productive spaces and time to access the products. Finally, contributions related to risk regulation—such as temperature regulation, flood mitigation, or water quality—are largely constrained by the availability and quality of green and natural spaces, as well as by a lack of knowledge about where these functions are provided.

Inequalities in access and impacts on quality of life

Men, people living in rural areas, and those with higher incomes and good physical health report fewer barriers and greater satisfaction with their access to nature and the benefits they derive from it. With the exception of income, these factors are also associated with a stronger sense of connection to nature.

Taken together, these determinants contribute—directly or indirectly, through access barriers and levels of connection to nature—to an overall lower quality of life for urban populations and for more socially vulnerable groups, particularly women, people with reduced mobility, and low-income households.

The study has been published online as a preprint and is currently under review.
